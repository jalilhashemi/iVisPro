Changes in foreign resident population in the city of Zürich

The story of Zürich as a history of immigration

When Zürich developed into a metropolis at the turn of the 20th century due to industrialization and consequent rural depopulation, immigrants played a major role in construction. Hence, by the year 1912 immigrants represented 34.2% of Zurich’s population.

During World War I and II many of these immigrants left Zurich again, because men were conscripted for armed service in their home countries. The weak economy and restrictive asylum politics after World War I did the rest. 1945, at the end of World War II foreign resident population in Zurich amounted to only 6.8%.

Since the Swiss industry and infrastructure survived World War II relatively intact, the economic boom in the 50’s lead to a turnaround. Due to the increased demand of working force more immigrants moved to Zurich again. Entire neighborhoods transformed into multicultural melting pots. In the 50’s many working immigrants came from Italy and by 1970 almost every second immigrant hold an Italian passport .

In the following decades immigration remained at high levels and the higher birth rate of immigrant women compared to Swiss women contributed to a growing percentage of foreign resident population in Zurich. In the 80’s and 90’s the civil wars in Sri Lanka and Ex-Yugoslavia caused many families to flee to Zurich.

Nowadays, immigrants of 171 different nationalities make up 32.4% of Zurich’s resident population, most of them originating from European countries. Since the entry into force of the Agreement on the Free Movement of Persons in 2002, increasingly single individuals with a higher level of education from EU/EFTA member states moved to Zurich for work purposes. German citizens currently represent almost a quarter of the foreign resident population. Contrary to frequent assumptions asylum seekers only represent a very small percentage of the foreign resident population of Zurich.
